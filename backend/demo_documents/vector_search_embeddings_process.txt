HOW VECTOR SEARCH & EMBEDDINGS WORK
A Complete Step-by-Step Breakdown, From Raw Text to Search Results



INTRODUCTION

Vector search is the technology that powers modern semantic
search, recommendation engines, AI chatbots with memory,
image search, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems.
Unlike traditional keyword search, which matches literal words,
vector search matches meaning. It does this by converting text,
images, or other data into long lists of numbers called
"embeddings," then finding which of those number-lists are
mathematically closest to each other in space. This document
breaks the entire pipeline down into clear, numbered steps, each
with a title and a detailed breakdown, starting from raw data all
the way through to the final ranked search results a user sees.



STEP 1: UNDERSTANDING THE CORE PROBLEM

Before any math happens, it helps to understand why this
technology exists in the first place.

BREAKDOWN:
- Traditional keyword search (like an old-school Ctrl+F or basic
  database query) only matches exact or near-exact words. If you
  search "car" it won't find a document that only says
  "automobile," even though the meaning is nearly identical.
- Human language is full of synonyms, paraphrasing, and context.
  A search engine that only understands literal strings of
  letters misses the actual intent behind a query.
- Vector search solves this by representing meaning numerically.
  Words, sentences, or entire documents with similar meaning end
  up as numbers that are mathematically "close" to each other,
  regardless of whether they share any exact words at all.
- The core idea: convert everything (queries and documents) into
  points in a high-dimensional mathematical space, then measure
  distance between those points to determine similarity.



STEP 2: TOKENIZATION — BREAKING TEXT INTO PIECES

Before a computer can turn text into numbers, it first has to
break that text down into smaller, manageable units.

BREAKDOWN:
- Raw text (a sentence, paragraph, or document) is split into
  "tokens," which can be whole words, parts of words (subwords),
  or even individual characters, depending on the tokenizer used.
- Modern systems typically use subword tokenization methods, such
  as Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) or WordPiece, which break rare or
  complex words into smaller, more common fragments. For example,
  "unbelievable" might become "un" + "believ" + "able."
- This approach lets the model handle words it has never seen
  before by reconstructing them from familiar smaller pieces,
  rather than treating every unseen word as a total mystery.
- Each token is then mapped to a unique numerical ID using a
  fixed vocabulary the model was trained with, essentially a
  giant lookup table matching token fragments to ID numbers.
- The output of this step is not yet a meaningful representation
  of the text's meaning; it's simply a sequence of ID numbers
  ready to be fed into the embedding model.



STEP 3: GENERATING THE EMBEDDING — TURNING TOKENS INTO NUMBERS

This is the heart of the entire system: converting tokenized text
into a dense vector of numbers that captures meaning.

BREAKDOWN:
- The sequence of token IDs is passed into a neural network,
  typically a Transformer-based encoder model (such as BERT-style
  architectures, or specialized embedding models like OpenAI's
  text-embedding models, Cohere's embed models, or open-source
  models like sentence-transformers).
- The neural network was trained on massive amounts of text, and
  during that training, it learned to place words and sentences
  with similar meaning near each other in a mathematical space,
  and dissimilar meanings far apart.
- Internally, each token is first converted into an initial
  "token embedding" using a learned embedding layer, then those
  token embeddings pass through multiple Transformer layers that
  use a mechanism called "self-attention" to let every token
  consider the context of every other token in the sequence. This
  is how the model understands that "bank" means something
  different in "river bank" versus "savings bank."
- After passing through all the network's layers, the model
  produces one final numerical vector, typically by averaging or
  pooling the token-level outputs (a method called "mean
  pooling"), or by using a special designated summary token (often
  called a [CLS] token) whose output is treated as the
  representation of the entire input.
- The final result is a single fixed-length list of numbers,
  commonly ranging from 384 to 3072 numbers long depending on the
  model, called an "embedding vector" or simply an "embedding."
  This vector is the mathematical fingerprint of the input's
  meaning.



STEP 4: UNDERSTANDING WHAT THE NUMBERS ACTUALLY MEAN

It helps to understand conceptually what these numbers represent,
even though no single number has an obvious human meaning on its
own.

BREAKDOWN:
- Each embedding is a point in a high-dimensional space. If an
  embedding has 768 numbers, it represents a single point in a
  768-dimensional space, something impossible for humans to
  visualize directly, but perfectly normal for computers to work
  with mathematically.
- No individual number in the vector corresponds to a specific,
  human-readable concept like "is about animals" or "is
  positive in tone." Instead, meaning is encoded across
  combinations of many numbers simultaneously, in patterns learned
  automatically during training.
- What matters is the relationship between vectors, not the
  values themselves. Two sentences with similar meaning will
  produce vectors that point in similar directions in that
  high-dimensional space, even if the raw numbers look completely
  different at a glance.
- A useful (simplified) mental model: imagine a 2D map where
  "dog" and "puppy" are plotted close together, "car" and
  "automobile" are plotted close together, but "dog" and "car"
  are plotted far apart. Real embeddings do this same thing, just
  across hundreds or thousands of dimensions instead of two.



STEP 5: MEASURING SIMILARITY BETWEEN VECTORS

Once you have embeddings, you need a mathematical way to measure
how "close" two of them are.

BREAKDOWN:
- Cosine similarity is the most common method. It measures the
  angle between two vectors rather than their raw distance,
  producing a score typically between -1 and 1, where 1 means the
  vectors point in an identical direction (very similar meaning),
  0 means they're unrelated, and -1 means they point in opposite
  directions (opposite meaning).
- Euclidean distance is another option, measuring the straight-
  line distance between two points in the vector space, similar
  to using the Pythagorean theorem in many dimensions at once.
  Smaller distance means more similar meaning.
- Dot product similarity is a simpler calculation often used when
  vectors have been "normalized" (scaled to a consistent length),
  in which case it produces results very similar to cosine
  similarity but is computationally cheaper to calculate at scale.
- The choice of similarity metric usually depends on which metric
  the embedding model was specifically trained and optimized to
  work with; using the wrong metric for a given model can produce
  noticeably worse results.



STEP 6: STORING EMBEDDINGS IN A VECTOR DATABASE

Generating one embedding is easy; the real challenge is storing
and searching through millions or billions of them efficiently.

BREAKDOWN:
- Each piece of source content (a document, a paragraph, an
  image, a product description) is converted into an embedding
  vector and stored alongside a reference back to the original
  content, often called "metadata" (such as a document ID, title,
  URL, or timestamp).
- Specialized vector databases (examples include Pinecone,
  Weaviate, Milvus, Qdrant, and Chroma) or vector-search
  extensions built into traditional databases (like pgvector for
  PostgreSQL) are used to store these vectors efficiently.
- Storing embeddings is simple in theory (just a list of numbers
  per item), but the real engineering challenge is searching
  through enormous quantities of them quickly, which ordinary
  brute-force comparison would make painfully slow at scale.
- This is why vector databases use specialized indexing
  structures, covered in the next step, rather than simply
  comparing a query vector against every stored vector one by
  one.



STEP 7: BUILDING AN INDEX FOR FAST APPROXIMATE SEARCH

Comparing a query against millions or billions of stored vectors
one at a time (called "brute-force" or "exact" search) is
accurate but far too slow for real-world applications, so vector
databases use clever indexing shortcuts instead.

BREAKDOWN:
- HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) is one of the most
  widely used indexing algorithms. It builds a multi-layered graph
  structure connecting similar vectors to each other, allowing
  searches to "hop" quickly toward the right neighborhood of the
  vector space instead of scanning everything.
- IVF (Inverted File Index) works by first clustering all stored
  vectors into groups (using a technique similar to k-means
  clustering), then, at search time, only comparing the query
  against vectors in the most relevant clusters rather than the
  entire dataset.
- Product Quantization (PQ) is a compression technique that
  shrinks large vectors into smaller, approximate representations,
  trading a small amount of accuracy for major savings in memory
  usage and search speed, and is often combined with IVF.
- These indexing methods are collectively known as Approximate
  Nearest Neighbor (ANN) search, because they sacrifice a tiny
  amount of accuracy in exchange for massive speed improvements,
  typically finding results that are 95 to 99+ percent as accurate
  as an exhaustive brute-force search, but orders of magnitude
  faster.
- The choice of indexing algorithm involves tradeoffs between
  search speed, memory usage, accuracy, and how easily the index
  can be updated when new data is added.



STEP 8: EMBEDDING THE USER'S QUERY

When a user actually performs a search, their query has to go
through the exact same transformation process as the stored
content.

BREAKDOWN:
- The user's typed query (for example, "affordable running shoes
  for flat feet") is tokenized using the same tokenizer used
  during the original data indexing process.
- That tokenized query is passed through the exact same embedding
  model used to embed all the stored content, producing a single
  query embedding vector.
- It's critical that the same embedding model is used for both
  the stored data and the incoming query; mixing embeddings from
  two different models produces vectors that aren't comparable to
  each other, since different models place meaning in
  differently-structured spaces.
- Some systems apply lightweight query preprocessing before
  embedding, such as removing filler words, correcting typos, or
  expanding abbreviations, to improve embedding quality before the
  actual vector is generated.



STEP 9: SEARCHING THE INDEX FOR NEAREST NEIGHBORS

With a query embedding in hand, the system now searches the
vector index to find the closest matching stored vectors.

BREAKDOWN:
- The query vector is compared against the vector index built in
  Step 7, using the chosen similarity metric (cosine similarity,
  dot product, or Euclidean distance) from Step 5.
- Rather than comparing against every single stored vector, the
  ANN index intelligently narrows the search to the most
  promising regions of the vector space first, dramatically
  reducing computation time.
- The system returns the "k nearest neighbors," meaning the top
  "k" closest vectors to the query, where k is a number chosen by
  the application (for example, the top 10 or top 50 most similar
  results).
- Each returned result includes a similarity score alongside the
  reference metadata (like the original document ID or URL),
  allowing the system to trace each vector match back to its
  original source content.



STEP 10: HYBRID SEARCH — COMBINING VECTORS WITH KEYWORDS

Many real-world systems don't rely on vector search alone; they
blend it with traditional keyword search for better results.

BREAKDOWN:
- Pure vector search excels at understanding meaning and intent
  but can sometimes miss exact matches that matter, such as
  specific product codes, names, or rare technical terms that the
  embedding model may not represent distinctly.
- Traditional keyword search methods, such as BM25 (a statistical
  ranking algorithm based on word frequency), excel at precise
  literal matching but miss semantic relationships entirely.
- Hybrid search runs both methods in parallel, then combines their
  results using a scoring formula (often called "reciprocal rank
  fusion" or a weighted blend) that balances semantic relevance
  with exact keyword matching.
- This hybrid approach tends to outperform either method used
  alone, and is widely used in production search systems,
  including many modern e-commerce and enterprise search
  platforms.



STEP 11: RE-RANKING THE TOP RESULTS

After the initial fast retrieval step narrows millions of items
down to a shortlist, many systems apply a second, more precise
pass to re-order those results.

BREAKDOWN:
- Initial vector search retrieval is optimized for speed across
  huge datasets, which sometimes sacrifices a small amount of
  precision in exchange for that speed.
- A "re-ranker" model, often a more powerful but slower neural
  network called a cross-encoder, is applied only to the smaller
  shortlist of top candidates (for example, the top 50 results),
  since running it against the entire dataset would be far too
  slow.
- Unlike the embedding model, which processes the query and each
  document completely separately, a cross-encoder re-ranker looks
  at the query and each candidate document together at the same
  time, allowing it to capture more nuanced relationships and
  produce a more accurate relevance score.
- The re-ranker reorders the shortlist based on this more precise
  scoring, and this refined order is typically what gets shown to
  the end user, or passed onward to a language model in
  retrieval-augmented generation systems.



STEP 12: RETURNING AND USING THE FINAL RESULTS

The final step is delivering these results to wherever they're
actually needed, whether that's a search results page or an AI
model's context window.

BREAKDOWN:
- In a typical semantic search product, the final ranked results
  are displayed to the user as a list, often alongside snippets of
  the original text pulled from the metadata stored back in Step
  6.
- In retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems, commonly used
  by AI chatbots and assistants, the top retrieved documents are
  instead inserted directly into the prompt sent to a large
  language model, giving that model relevant, up-to-date, or
  private information it wouldn't otherwise have access to from
  its training data alone.
- In recommendation systems, the retrieved "nearest neighbor"
  items (such as similar products or similar songs) are presented
  to users as suggestions, based on the same underlying vector
  similarity principles.
- Regardless of the specific application, the fundamental cycle
  remains the same: convert data into meaningful numerical
  vectors, store those vectors in a searchable structure, convert
  incoming queries into the same kind of vector, and then
  mathematically compare vectors to surface the most relevant
  matches.



BONUS: KEY TERMS QUICK-REFERENCE GLOSSARY

- EMBEDDING: A fixed-length list of numbers representing the
  meaning of a piece of text, image, or other data.
- VECTOR SPACE: The high-dimensional mathematical space where all
  embeddings exist as points.
- COSINE SIMILARITY: A measurement of the angle between two
  vectors, commonly used to measure how similar their meanings
  are.
- ANN (APPROXIMATE NEAREST NEIGHBOR): A family of fast search
  algorithms that trade a small amount of accuracy for major
  speed gains at scale.
- HNSW: A graph-based indexing algorithm widely used in modern
  vector databases for fast approximate search.
- RAG (RETRIEVAL-AUGMENTED GENERATION): A technique where an AI
  language model's prompt is enriched with relevant documents
  retrieved through vector search before generating a response.
- RE-RANKING: A secondary, more precise scoring pass applied to a
  small shortlist of already-retrieved results to improve final
  ordering.
- HYBRID SEARCH: Combining vector-based semantic search with
  traditional keyword-based search for more balanced, accurate
  results.
