A Deep Dive Into the History, Products, and Culture of Apple Inc.


introduction

Apple Inc. is one of the most influential companies in the history
of technology, a business that has repeatedly reshaped how people
listen to music, make phone calls, work, communicate, and think
about design itself. Founded in a garage in Los Altos, California,
in 1976, Apple grew from a small hobbyist computer company into a
global technology giant valued at close to five trillion dollars
as of mid-2026, trading places with Nvidia and a handful of other
tech titans for the title of the world's most valuable public
company. Apple's story is inseparable from the story of its
co-founder Steve Jobs, whose obsession with simplicity, industrial
design, and "insanely great" products became the company's guiding
philosophy long after his death in 2011. Under current CEO Tim
Cook, who took over from Jobs, Apple has expanded far beyond
computers and phones into services, wearables, and a tightly
integrated ecosystem that keeps hundreds of millions of users
inside its walled garden. Below is a wide-ranging essay-style
breakdown of Apple's history, followed by ninety standalone facts
covering everything from the founding of the company to the inner
workings of iOS, that can be used as trivia, conversation starters,
or general reference material.


PART ONE: THE ESSAY — HOW APPLE BECAME Apple

Apple's origin story is legendary in Silicon Valley folklore.
Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne founded Apple Computer
Company on April 1, 1976. Wozniak was the engineering genius who
built the Apple I by hand, while Jobs was the visionary who saw
the potential to sell fully assembled computers to a market that,
at the time, was dominated by hobbyist kits. Ronald Wayne, the
lesser-known third co-founder, sold his 10 percent stake back to
Jobs and Wozniak for just 800 dollars less than two weeks after
the company was formed, a decision that would have made him a
multi-billionaire had he held on. The Apple II, released in 1977,
became one of the first mass-produced personal computers and
helped kickstart the home computer revolution, giving Apple the
capital and credibility to go public in 1980 in what was, at the
time, the biggest IPO since Ford Motor Company in 1956.

The 1980s brought both triumph and turmoil. The Macintosh,
launched in 1984 with a now-iconic Ridley Scott-directed Super
Bowl commercial referencing George Orwell's "1984," introduced the
graphical user interface and mouse to the mainstream, borrowing
heavily from technology first demonstrated at Xerox PARC. Despite
the cultural splash, internal power struggles between Jobs and
CEO John Sculley led to Jobs being pushed out of his own company
in 1985. Jobs went on to found NeXT, a computer company aimed at
higher education and business, and separately purchased a small
computer graphics division from Lucasfilm that he renamed Pixar,
which would go on to revolutionize animated filmmaking. Meanwhile,
Apple without Jobs drifted through the late 1980s and 1990s,
releasing a scattered lineup of products, including the Newton
MessagePad, an early and commercially unsuccessful personal
digital assistant.

Apple's fortunes changed dramatically in 1996-1997. Struggling
financially and lacking a modern operating system, Apple acquired
NeXT for approximately 400 million dollars, a deal that brought
Jobs back into the fold as an advisor and, soon after, as interim
CEO. NeXT's operating system, NeXTSTEP, became the technical
foundation for what would eventually become Mac OS X (and later,
macOS), as well as the underlying architecture for iOS. Jobs
famously secured a 150 million dollar investment from rival
Microsoft in 1997 to keep Apple solvent, a deal that also ensured
Microsoft Office would continue to be developed for the Mac. From
this position of near-collapse, Apple began one of the most
remarkable corporate turnarounds in business history.

The late 1990s and 2000s saw a string of category-defining
products. The colorful iMac G3, released in 1998, ditched floppy
disks and legacy ports in favor of USB, and its translucent
"Bondi Blue" design signaled Apple's return to bold, human-
centered design. The iPod, launched in 2001, transformed the
consumics of the music industry alongside the iTunes Store,
which launched in 2003 and legitimized paid digital music
downloads at a time when piracy was rampant. Apple's marketing
slogan "1,000 songs in your pocket" captured the cultural moment
perfectly. But it was the iPhone, unveiled by Jobs on January 9,
2007, and released on June 29, 2007, that truly redefined the
company and the entire consumer electronics industry. Jobs
described it as three devices in one: a widescreen iPod with
touch controls, a revolutionary mobile phone, and a breakthrough
internet communications device. The App Store followed in July
2008, creating an entirely new economy of third-party software
developers and cementing the iPhone's dominance.

The iPad arrived in 2010, carving out the modern tablet market
almost single-handedly, though critics at the time dismissed it
as "just a big iPod touch." Steve Jobs passed away on October 5,
2011, from complications related to pancreatic cancer, just one
day after Apple unveiled the iPhone 4s. Tim Cook, who had served
as Apple's Chief Operating Officer and had already been filling
in for Jobs during his medical leaves, took over as CEO. Under
Cook's leadership, Apple has emphasized operational excellence,
supply chain mastery, privacy as a marketing pillar, and a
dramatic pivot toward high-margin services such as Apple Music,
iCloud, Apple TV+, Apple Arcade, and Apple Pay. Cook has also
overseen the introduction of the Apple Watch in 2015, AirPods in
2016, and the company's own silicon transition, moving Macs from
Intel processors to Apple-designed ARM-based M-series chips
starting in 2020 with the M1.

iOS itself has evolved enormously since its 2007 debut, when it
didn't even have an official name; it was originally called
"iPhone OS." The software introduced the App Store in 2008 (iOS
2), multitasking and folders in iOS 4, Siri in iOS 5, Apple Maps
(replacing Google Maps as default) in iOS 6, a complete visual
overhaul under design chief Jony Ive in iOS 7, widgets and a
redesigned home screen in later versions, Dark Mode in iOS 13,
App Tracking Transparency privacy controls in iOS 14.5, and
customizable lock screens in iOS 16. Each release reflects
Apple's broader strategic priorities, whether that's opening up
to developers, tightening privacy, or deepening integration
across the Apple ecosystem of iPhone, iPad, Mac, Watch, and
Apple TV.

Today, Apple is as much a services and ecosystem company as it
is a hardware company. Billions of active devices are in use
worldwide, tied together by iCloud syncing, Handoff, Continuity,
and Apple's tightly controlled hardware-software integration.
Apple's massive cash reserves, aggressive stock buyback programs,
and enormous profit margins make it a perennial fixture at or
near the top of global market capitalization rankings. It remains
headquartered at Apple Park, the ring-shaped campus in Cupertino,
California, designed with input from Jobs himself before his
death and completed in 2017. Whether admired for its design
philosophy or criticized for its walled-garden approach, high
prices, and right-to-repair battles, Apple's influence on modern
computing, communication, and consumer culture is undeniable.


PART TWO: 90 APPLE & iOS Facts

COMPANY HISTORY & FOUNDING

1. Apple was founded on April 1, 1976, by Steve Jobs, Steve
   Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne.

2. Ronald Wayne sold his 10 percent stake in Apple for 800
   dollars just twelve days after the company was founded.

3. The Apple I was hand-built by Steve Wozniak and sold as a
   circuit board without a case, keyboard, or monitor.

4. Apple was originally headquartered in Steve Jobs's parents'
   garage in Los Altos, California.

5. The company's name reportedly came from Jobs's time working
   at an apple orchard, and possibly his fondness for the fruit,
   though the exact origin has been debated over the years.

6. Apple's first logo, designed in 1976, featured Isaac Newton
   sitting under an apple tree, and was quickly replaced by the
   simpler rainbow-striped apple logo in 1977.

7. Apple went public on December 12, 1980, creating more
   instant millionaires (over 300) than any company in history
   up to that point.

8. The Macintosh, launched in 1984, was introduced with a
   famous Super Bowl commercial directed by Ridley Scott.

9. Steve Jobs was forced out of Apple in 1985 following internal
   conflicts with CEO John Sculley.

10. After leaving Apple, Jobs founded NeXT and also purchased
    the computer graphics division of Lucasfilm, renaming it
    Pixar Animation Studios.

11. Apple acquired NeXT in 1996 for roughly 400 million dollars,
    bringing Steve Jobs back to the company.

12. Jobs became Apple's interim CEO in 1997 and dropped the
    "interim" from his title in 2000.

13. In 1997, rival Microsoft invested 150 million dollars in
    Apple to help keep the struggling company afloat.

14. Apple's official corporate name was "Apple Computer, Inc."
    until 2007, when it was shortened to "Apple Inc." to reflect
    its expansion beyond computers.

15. Steve Jobs died on October 5, 2011, one day after Apple
    announced the iPhone 4s.

16. Tim Cook became Apple's CEO in August 2011 and previously
    served as the company's Chief Operating Officer.

17. Apple Park, the company's ring-shaped headquarters in
    Cupertino, opened in 2017 and was one of the last projects
    Steve Jobs personally helped design.

18. Apple became the first publicly traded U.S. company to
    reach a 1 trillion dollar market valuation in August 2018.

19. Apple later became the first U.S. company to reach a 2
    trillion dollar valuation in August 2020.

20. As of mid-2026, Apple's market capitalization has fluctuated
    in the range of roughly 3.7 to nearly 5 trillion dollars,
    keeping it among the one or two most valuable companies in
    the world alongside Nvidia.


HARDWARE MILESTONES

21. The Apple II, released in 1977, was one of the first
    successful mass-produced personal computers.

22. The original Macintosh, released in 1984, was the first
    mass-market computer to popularize the graphical user
    interface and mouse for everyday consumers.

23. The iMac G3, launched in 1998, came in translucent colors
    and helped revive Apple's fortunes with its bold design.

24. The iMac G3 dropped the floppy disk drive entirely in favor
    of USB ports, a controversial move at the time.

25. The iPod was released on October 23, 2001, and was marketed
    with the slogan "1,000 songs in your pocket."

26. The first iPods used a FireWire connection and a mechanical
    scroll wheel before later models introduced the touch-
    sensitive click wheel.

27. The iPhone was unveiled by Steve Jobs on January 9, 2007,
    and went on sale June 29, 2007.

28. Jobs introduced the original iPhone as three devices in one:
    an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator.

29. The first iPhone did not support third-party apps or copy
    and paste; both features came in later software updates.

30. The iPad was introduced in January 2010 and helped define
    the modern consumer tablet category.

31. The Apple Watch was announced in September 2014 and released
    in April 2015, marking Apple's entry into wearables.

32. AirPods were introduced in 2016 as a wireless replacement for
    Apple's iconic wired earbuds, launching alongside the removal
    of the headphone jack from the iPhone 7.

33. Apple began transitioning Mac computers from Intel processors
    to its own custom-designed ARM-based Apple Silicon chips in
    2020, starting with the M1 chip.

34. The Apple Silicon transition allowed iPhone and iPad apps to
    run natively on Mac computers for the first time.

35. Apple has used several different processor architectures over
    its history, moving from the Motorola 68000 series to
    PowerPC, then to Intel x86, and finally to its own ARM-based
    Apple Silicon.

36. The Touch Bar, a thin OLED strip replacing the function key
    row, debuted on the MacBook Pro in 2016 and was eventually
    phased out on later redesigns.

37. Apple's Lightning connector, introduced in 2012, was
    eventually phased out in favor of USB-C starting with the
    iPhone 15 in 2023, driven partly by European Union
    regulations.

38. Face ID, introduced with the iPhone X in 2017, replaced Touch
    ID's fingerprint sensor with facial recognition using an
    infrared depth-sensing camera system.

39. Touch ID, Apple's fingerprint recognition system, first
    appeared on the iPhone 5s in 2013.

40. The iPhone X, released in 2017, was the first iPhone to
    remove the physical home button in favor of full-screen
    gesture navigation.


iOS & SOFTWARE

41. iOS was originally called "iPhone OS" and did not receive
    its current name until iOS 4 in 2010.

42. The App Store officially launched on July 10, 2008, with iOS
    2.0 and around 500 apps available at launch.

43. Multitasking for third-party apps was not introduced until
    iOS 4 in 2010; earlier versions could only run one app at a
    time.

44. Siri, Apple's voice assistant, was introduced with the iPhone
    4s in 2011 and was based on technology Apple acquired from a
    startup of the same name.

45. Apple Maps replaced Google Maps as the default mapping app in
    iOS 6 in 2012, a launch widely criticized for inaccurate data
    at the time.

46. iOS 7, released in 2013 under design chief Jony Ive, brought
    a dramatic visual overhaul, moving away from skeuomorphic
    design toward a flatter, more minimalist interface.

47. Apple Pay launched in 2014, allowing contactless payments
    using near-field communication (NFC) technology built into
    iPhones.

48. Dark Mode was introduced system-wide in iOS 13, released in
    2019.

49. App Tracking Transparency, introduced in iOS 14.5 in 2021,
    requires apps to ask users for permission before tracking
    their activity across other apps and websites.

50. iOS 16, released in 2022, introduced a highly customizable
    lock screen with widgets and multiple photo styles.

51. iOS is built on a Unix-based foundation called Darwin, which
    is also the open-source core underlying macOS.

52. iOS and macOS share a common technical lineage that traces
    back to NeXTSTEP, the operating system Apple acquired along
    with NeXT in 1996.

53. Swift, Apple's modern programming language for iOS and macOS
    app development, was introduced in 2014 as a faster, safer
    alternative to Objective-C.

54. Objective-C was the primary programming language for Apple
    software development for decades before Swift's introduction.

55. iCloud, Apple's cloud storage and sync service, launched in
    2011, replacing the earlier MobileMe service.

56. Handoff and Continuity features, introduced in 2014, allow
    users to start a task on one Apple device and seamlessly
    continue it on another.

57. AirDrop, introduced in 2011, allows quick file sharing
    between nearby Apple devices using a combination of Bluetooth
    and Wi-Fi.

58. Apple's Find My network uses a crowdsourced mesh of hundreds
    of millions of Apple devices to help locate lost devices and
    AirTags, even when they're offline.

59. FaceTime, Apple's video calling app, launched in 2010
    alongside the iPhone 4, which was the first iPhone with a
    front-facing camera.

60. Emoji were first included on iPhones starting with iOS 2.2 in
    2008, though initially the keyboard was hidden and required
    third-party apps to unlock in some regions.


CULTURE, BUSINESS & MISCELLANEOUS

61. Apple's fiscal year ends in late September, so its quarterly
    earnings calendar does not match the standard calendar year.

62. Apple typically unveils new iPhones each September, a
    tradition dating back to the iPhone 5 in 2012 (earlier
    iPhones launched in the summer).

63. The "i" prefix in iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad was popularized
    by Steve Jobs, though Apple has never officially confirmed a
    single definitive meaning, with "internet" being the most
    commonly cited inspiration.

64. Apple's advertising slogan "Think Different," launched in
    1997, was created by ad agency TBWA\Chiat\Day and featured
    historical figures rather than Apple products.

65. Jony Ive served as Apple's Chief Design Officer for over two
    decades before departing the company in 2019 to start his own
    independent design firm, LoveFrom.

66. Apple has consistently ranked among the world's most valuable
    brands in rankings by firms such as Interbrand.

67. Apple Park's main building is a circular structure often
    nicknamed "the spaceship" and is covered in curved glass
    panels.

68. Apple manufactures the vast majority of its hardware through
    contract manufacturers, most notably Foxconn (Hon Hai
    Precision Industry), primarily in China, though Apple has
    increasingly diversified assembly to countries like India and
    Vietnam.

69. Apple's Ireland-based subsidiary structure became the subject
    of a major European Commission tax ruling, which found in
    2016 that Apple owed Ireland billions in unpaid taxes; the
    case went through years of appeals in European courts.

70. Apple has faced ongoing antitrust scrutiny worldwide over App
    Store policies, including a high-profile legal battle with
    Epic Games over in-app payment rules that began in 2020.

71. The European Union's Digital Markets Act has forced Apple to
    make significant changes in the EU, including allowing
    alternative app marketplaces and switching to USB-C charging.

72. Apple's "walled garden" approach, which limits apps to
    Apple-approved distribution channels on iOS, is often cited
    as both a security strength and a competitive point of
    criticism.

73. Apple has topped Fortune's list of most admired companies
    multiple times over the past two decades.

74. The Apple logo has never included the company's name since
    1998, relying entirely on the bitten apple silhouette for
    brand recognition.

75. The "bite" in the Apple logo is often said to help
    distinguish the silhouette from a cherry or tomato at small
    sizes, though Apple has never given one single official
    explanation.

76. Apple's original mouse for the Macintosh in 1984 had only one
    button, a design philosophy Steve Jobs favored for
    simplicity, unlike the multi-button mice common on PCs.

77. Apple has one of the largest cash reserves of any public
    company in the world, historically holding well over 100
    billion dollars in cash and marketable securities.

78. Apple Card, a credit card launched in partnership with
    Goldman Sachs in 2019, was one of Apple's first major moves
    into consumer financial services.

79. Apple TV+ launched in November 2019 and has since won
    multiple major awards, including Best Picture at the Oscars
    for "CODA" in 2022, the first streaming service to do so for
    Apple.

80. Apple Arcade, a subscription gaming service offering ad-free
    and download-free access to a curated library of games,
    launched in 2019.

81. Apple's various subscription offerings, including Apple
    Music, iCloud+, Apple TV+, and Apple Arcade, can be bundled
    together through a subscription plan called Apple One.

82. The Newton MessagePad, released in 1993, was one of Apple's
    earliest attempts at a handheld personal digital assistant
    and was ultimately discontinued in 1998.

83. Apple briefly experimented with licensing its Mac operating
    system to third-party manufacturers to build "Mac clones" in
    the mid-1990s, a program Steve Jobs ended shortly after his
    return.

84. The Apple Newton's handwriting recognition software became a
    popular target for jokes and satire, most famously in the
    comic strip Doonesbury.

85. Apple's retail stores, which began opening in 2001, are known
    for their minimalist glass-and-steel designs and helped
    redefine consumer electronics retail.

86. The Apple Store on Fifth Avenue in New York City is famous
    for its all-glass cube entrance above an underground store.

87. Apple's environmental initiatives include a stated goal of
    achieving carbon neutrality across its entire supply chain
    and product life cycle by 2030.

88. Apple has released a "Self Service Repair" program, allowing
    customers to purchase official parts and tools to repair
    certain devices themselves, partly in response to
    right-to-repair pressure.

89. Vision Pro, Apple's mixed-reality headset, was unveiled in
    2023 and released in early 2024, marking the company's first
    entirely new product category since the Apple Watch.

90. Apple's product naming and release conventions, its annual
    Worldwide Developers Conference (WWDC) each June, and its
    September hardware events have become fixtures of the tech
    calendar that are closely watched by media, investors, and
    consumers around the world every year.
