FACT #1
Q: What was the internal codename for the original iPhone project at Apple?

A: The original iPhone project was developed under the secret codename 'Project Purple' (or 'Purple 2') starting around 2004. Apple engineers worked in a highly restricted facility nicknamed the 'Purple Dorm' on the Cupertino campus, where access was strictly controlled with keycards and security cameras. The room even featured a poster that said 'Fight Club' because the first rule of Project Purple was to never talk about Project Purple.

FACT #2
Q: Why did Steve Jobs choose 9:41 AM as the default time displayed on iPhone screens in marketing materials?

A: When Steve Jobs introduced the original iPhone in January 2007, he timed his keynote presentation so that the big reveal of the device would happen roughly 40 minutes into the talk. To make the rendered device screen on the giant backdrop match the real clock on attendees' watches, Apple set the display time to 9:42 AM. Later, with the introduction of the iPad in 2010, the official reveal hit precisely at 9:41 AM, which became the permanent standardized clock setting for all future Apple product imagery.


FACT #3
Q: How did Apple secure the 'iPhone' trademark despite Cisco already owning it?

A: Cisco Systems actually held the registered trademark for 'iPhone' for a line of VoIP home phones manufactured by its Linksys division. Apple launched the original iPhone in January 2007 without having finalized a trademark transfer agreement with Cisco, leading Cisco to file a lawsuit immediately. However, within a few weeks, both companies reached a settlement agreement allowing Apple to use the name globally in exchange for crosslicensing technology and collaborating on interoperability.


FACT #4
Q: What was the famous 'magic trick' Steve Jobs performed during the 2007 iPhone launch to prevent crashes?

A: The prototype iPhone running during the legendary 2007 keynote was so unstable that it could only execute specific actions in a strict sequence—known internally as the 'golden path'—without running out of RAM or crashing. Engineers rigged the phone to always display five full signal bars regardless of actual cell signal strength, and Steve Jobs had multiple prototype phones hidden under the podium so he could seamlessly switch to a fresh unit if one froze.


FACT #5
Q: What role did the iPod click wheel play in the early development of iOS?

A: Before settling on the multitouch capacitive touchscreen interface, Apple actively prototyped an iPhone based on the iPod's physical click wheel, known internally as 'Acorn OS'. Users had to scroll through contacts and dial numbers using a rotary dial on the screen, similar to an old rotary telephone. Steve Jobs quickly realized how frustrating and inefficient it was, leading Apple to pivot entirely to multitouch screen technology.


FACT #6
Q: Which company manufactured the central processing unit (CPU) for the very first iPhone?

A: Although Samsung is today widely viewed as Apple's primary smartphone competitor, Apple partnered with Samsung to manufacture the ARMbased systemonchip (SoC) for the original 2007 iPhone. Samsung produced these early processors before Apple acquired P.A. Semi in 2008 and began designing its own custom silicon series, starting with the Apple A4 chip in 2010.


FACT #7
Q: How did the iconic original glass screen on the iPhone come together at the last minute?

A: Just weeks before the original iPhone launch, Steve Jobs complained that carrying prototype units in his pocket with plastic screens caused scratches from his keys. He personally contacted Corning CEO Wendell Weeks to inquire about 'Gorilla Glass'—a durable, chemicalstrengthened glass Corning had developed in the 1960s but never commercialized. Corning reopened a factory to manufacture enough Gorilla Glass for the first production run in less than six weeks.


FACT #8

A: Before embarking on a touchscreen phone, Apple collaborated with Motorola and Cingular Wireless (now AT&T) in 2005 to create the Motorola ROKR E1. Dubbed the 'iTunes Phone', it was the first nonApple device to support iTunes playback, but it was heavily criticized for holding only 100 songs and featuring an awkward interface. The frustration over the ROKR motivated Steve Jobs to take full control of mobile design and initiate Project Purple 2.

FACT #20
Q: What is the 'Haptic Engine' in modern iPhones and how does it differ from traditional vibration motors?

A: Traditional smartphones used eccentric rotating mass (ERM) motors that vibrated continuously with slow spinup and spindown times. Apple's Taptic Engine uses a linear resonant actuator that moves a mass back and forth in precise, instant bursts. This allows iOS to generate tactile feedback mimicking physical buttons, spring clicks, keyboard taps, and subtle UI notifications.

FACT #38
Q: What is the purpose of the optical heart sensor's green LED lights on the back of Apple Watch?

A: Apple Watch uses Photoplethysmography (PPG) to track heart rate. Blood appears red because it reflects red light and absorbs green light. With every heart beat, blood flow spikes, absorbing more green light—allowing the watch to calculate heart beats per minute.

FACT #67
Q: How does 'Private Relay' in iCloud+ hide your browsing activity from ISPs and network trackers?

Neither Apple nor the website host can view both who you are and where you are browsing.

Q: Why did early iPod and iPhone prototypes feature hidden games like Breakout?

A: The original iPod included a hidden Breakout game as an Easter egg accessed by holding down the center button in the 'About' menu. This was a personal nod to Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak's history at Atari in 1976, where they designed the hardware circuit board for the original Breakout arcade game overnight before founding Apple Computer.

Q: What visual detail occurs on the iOS Flashlight control icon when you toggle it on?

A: When you swipe open the iOS Control Center and toggle the Flashlight on or off, the tiny physical switch rendered on the Flashlight icon visibly flips up into the 'ON' position, complete with a tiny glowing orange indicator light, and flips back down when turned off.


FACT #79
Q: How did Apple's iconic '1984' Super Bowl commercial shape tech culture forever?

A: Directed by Ridley Scott, Apple's famous '1984' Macintosh commercial aired during Super Bowl XVIII, depicting a heroine throwing a sledgehammer through a giant screen broadcasting a dystopian 'Big Brother' figure. The commercial framed Apple as the revolutionary force rescuing personal computing from corporate conformity, pioneering highconcept cinematic advertising for consumer tech.


FACT #80
Q: What is the significance of the text 'Designed by Apple in California' printed on product boxes?

A: Unlike most tech companies that emphasize manufacturing origins (such as 'Made in China'), Apple prominently engraves 'Designed by Apple in California' on product packaging, device hardware, and interior documentation. This phrase reinforces Apple's identity as a industrial design powerhouse centered in Silicon Valley, emphasizing software and hardware conceptualization regardless of where final assembly occurs.
