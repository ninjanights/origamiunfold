ENGINES & ELECTRIC VEHICLES: THE COMPLETE FACT FILE
A Deep Dive Into How Cars Move, Who Invented Them, and Where the
Industry Is Headed



INTRODUCTION

The story of how humans get from one place to another under their
own mechanical power is really two intertwined stories: the rise,
dominance, and slow decline of the internal combustion engine
(ICE), and the surprisingly old, then abandoned, then triumphantly
revived story of the electric vehicle (EV). Most people assume
electric cars are a 21st-century invention and gasoline cars came
first, but the truth is almost the opposite. Electric vehicles
were actually among the very first automobiles on the road in the
1800s, only to be pushed aside by gasoline power for most of the
20th century, before roaring back in the 21st century as battery
technology, environmental pressure, and companies like Tesla
changed the economics of driving. Today, in 2026, electric cars
account for roughly a quarter of all new car sales worldwide,
led overwhelmingly by China, with Europe close behind and the
United States lagging due to shifting federal incentives. Below
is a full essay covering the history and mechanics of both engine
types, followed by ninety standalone facts covering everything
from Karl Benz's patent to modern battery chemistry.


PART ONE: THE ESSAY — FROM STEAM TO SILICON


Long before gasoline engines existed, inventors were already
experimenting with electric propulsion. As early as the 1830s
and 1840s, tinkerers in Scotland, the Netherlands, and Hungary
built small electric carriages powered by non-rechargeable
batteries, decades before Karl Benz patented his gasoline-powered
Benz Patent-Motorwagen in 1886, generally considered the first
true automobile. By the late 1800s and into the early 1900s,
electric vehicles were genuinely competitive with, and in some
ways superior to, early gasoline cars. They were quiet, didn't
require hand-cranking to start, produced no exhaust fumes, and
were especially popular with urban drivers and women, since they
didn't require the physical strength needed to crank-start a
combustion engine. In fact, in the year 1900, roughly a third of
all cars on American roads were electric, competing directly
against steam-powered and gasoline-powered vehicles for market
share.

The combustion engine's triumph over electric power came down to
a combination of factors. Henry Ford's introduction of the moving
assembly line for the Model T in 1913 slashed the cost of
gasoline cars dramatically, making them far more affordable than
electric competitors, whose heavy lead-acid batteries remained
expensive and limited in range. The discovery of cheap Texas
crude oil lowered gasoline prices, and the invention of the
electric starter motor by Charles Kettering in 1912 eliminated
the dangerous hand-crank, removing one of the gasoline engine's
biggest disadvantages. By the 1920s, electric vehicles had all
but disappeared from the market, and the internal combustion
engine entered a dominance that would last for most of the
century. Meanwhile, the technology underlying the combustion
engine kept evolving, from Nikolaus Otto's four-stroke cycle in
1876, to Rudolf Diesel's compression-ignition engine patented in
1893, to the increasingly refined V8s, turbochargers, and fuel
injection systems that defined 20th-century automotive
engineering.

Electric vehicles resurfaced periodically throughout the
20th century, usually in response to oil shocks or environmental
concern, but never gained lasting traction. The 1973 oil crisis
spurred renewed interest, and General Motors' EV1, launched in
1996 and famously withdrawn and mostly destroyed by 2003, became
a cult symbol of a road not taken, later chronicled in the
documentary "Who Killed the Electric Car?" The real turning point
came with advances in lithium-ion battery technology, originally
developed for laptops and consumer electronics in the 1990s, and
with the founding of Tesla Motors in 2003 by Martin Eberhard and
Marc Tarpenning, with Elon Musk joining shortly after as an early
investor and eventually taking over as CEO. Tesla's Roadster,
launched in 2008, proved that an electric car could be genuinely
fast and desirable rather than a compromise, and the Model S,
launched in 2012, proved EVs could be practical luxury sedans
with real range. Legacy automakers, initially skeptical, followed
with their own electric offerings throughout the 2010s and 2020s,
while Chinese manufacturers, backed by aggressive government
industrial policy and companies like BYD, CATL, and NIO, turned
China into the largest EV market and battery-manufacturing hub on
Earth.

Mechanically, the internal combustion engine and the electric
motor work in fundamentally different ways. A gasoline or diesel
engine is fundamentally a controlled series of small explosions.
Air and fuel are mixed and compressed inside a cylinder, then
ignited, either by a spark plug in a gasoline engine or by the
heat of compression alone in a diesel engine. This explosion
pushes a piston downward, and that linear motion is converted
into rotational motion by a crankshaft. Most modern engines
repeat this across four strokes per cylinder: intake, compression,
combustion (power), and exhaust, a cycle formalized by Nikolaus
Otto in the 1860s and 1870s. The crankshaft's rotation eventually
turns the wheels through a transmission, which uses different
gear ratios to manage the tradeoff between torque and speed, since
combustion engines only produce useful power within a specific
range of engine speeds (measured in RPM). This is why gasoline
cars need multi-speed transmissions, clutches, and idling systems
in the first place; without gearing, the engine would either
stall at low speeds or scream uselessly at high ones.

Electric vehicles avoid nearly all of this mechanical complexity.
An electric motor works on electromagnetic principles discovered
by scientists like Michael Faraday in the 1830s: electric current
flowing through coils of wire creates a magnetic field, and that
field is arranged to interact with magnets (or induced currents)
on a rotating shaft, causing it to spin. Electric motors can
produce maximum torque instantly, from a full stop, which is why
EVs feel so immediately quick off the line compared to gasoline
cars, which need to build up RPM before delivering peak torque.
Because electric motors can operate efficiently across a much
wider range of speeds than combustion engines, most EVs get away
with a single-speed gear reduction instead of a traditional
multi-speed transmission. Power comes from a large battery pack,
typically composed of hundreds or thousands of individual lithium-
ion cells wired together, managed by a battery management system
that monitors temperature, charge balance, and safety. When you
brake in an EV, the motor can often run in reverse as a generator,
a process called regenerative braking, which slows the car while
recapturing kinetic energy and feeding it back into the battery,
something a conventional combustion car's brakes simply cannot do
since they only dissipate energy as heat through friction.

The two technologies also differ enormously in efficiency. A
typical gasoline engine converts only about 20 to 35 percent of
the energy stored in fuel into actual motion, with the rest lost
as heat and friction, which is why engines need radiators and
cooling systems in the first place. Electric motors, by contrast,
routinely convert 85 to 95 percent of stored electrical energy
into motion, making them dramatically more efficient machines,
even after accounting for losses in charging and battery
chemistry. This efficiency gap, combined with falling battery
prices, driven largely by manufacturing scale in China and
improvements in chemistries like lithium iron phosphate (LFP), is
the central reason EVs have become cost-competitive with gasoline
cars in a growing number of markets by the mid-2020s. As of 2026,
electric cars represent roughly a quarter of new car sales
worldwide, with China alone accounting for more than half of all
EVs sold globally, Europe growing steadily under tightening
emissions regulations, and the United States lagging behind after
federal EV tax credits were scaled back. Whether this trajectory
continues toward the IEA's projection of roughly half of global
car sales being electric by 2035 will depend on battery costs,
charging infrastructure, government policy, and how quickly
automakers can make EVs affordable for average buyers rather than
early adopters.


PART TWO: 90 ENGINE & EV FACTS

FOUNDING & EARLY HISTORY

1. Small-scale electric vehicles were built by inventors in
   Scotland, the Netherlands, and Hungary as early as the 1830s
   and 1840s, before gasoline cars existed.

2. Karl Benz patented the Benz Patent-Motorwagen in 1886,
   widely regarded as the first true gasoline-powered automobile.

3. Nikolaus Otto developed and patented the four-stroke internal
   combustion cycle in the 1860s and 1870s, forming the basis for
   most modern gasoline engines.

4. Rudolf Diesel patented his compression-ignition engine design
   in 1893, which does not require spark plugs and instead
   ignites fuel using the heat generated by compression alone.

5. By 1900, roughly one-third of cars on American roads were
   electric, competing directly against steam and gasoline
   vehicles.

6. Early electric cars were especially popular with women and
   urban drivers because they did not require dangerous hand-
   cranking to start, unlike gasoline vehicles of the era.

7. Henry Ford's moving assembly line, introduced for the Model T
   in 1913, dramatically lowered the cost of gasoline vehicles
   and helped push electric cars out of the mainstream market.

8. Charles Kettering invented the electric starter motor in
   1912, eliminating the need for hand-cranking gasoline engines
   and removing one of the EV's key advantages.

9. By the 1920s, electric vehicles had all but vanished from the
   consumer market, giving way almost entirely to gasoline power.

10. The 1973 oil crisis briefly revived interest in electric and
    alternative-fuel vehicles among researchers and automakers.

11. General Motors launched the EV1 in 1996, an early modern
    mass-produced electric car that was later withdrawn from the
    market and largely destroyed by 2003.

12. The EV1's discontinuation became the subject of the 2006
    documentary "Who Killed the Electric Car?"

13. Tesla Motors was founded in 2003 by engineers Martin Eberhard
    and Marc Tarpenning.

14. Elon Musk joined Tesla as an early investor and chairman in
    2004, later becoming CEO in 2008.

15. Tesla's first production car, the Roadster, launched in 2008
    and was based on a modified Lotus Elise chassis.

16. Tesla's Model S, launched in 2012, was the company's first
    car built entirely on its own platform and proved EVs could
    be practical, long-range vehicles.

17. Nissan's LEAF, launched in 2010, was one of the first mass-
    market, affordable electric cars from a major legacy
    automaker.

18. Chinese EV maker BYD originally began as a battery
    manufacturer in 1995 before expanding into vehicle
    production.

19. China's aggressive industrial policy and subsidies through
    the 2010s helped it become the world's largest EV
    manufacturing and battery-production hub.

20. By 2025, BYD had surpassed Tesla in total annual global EV
    deliveries, according to industry sales data.


HOW COMBUSTION ENGINES WORK

21. A gasoline engine works through a sequence of controlled
    small explosions inside enclosed cylinders.

22. Most gasoline and diesel engines use a four-stroke cycle:
    intake, compression, combustion (power), and exhaust.

23. In a gasoline engine, a spark plug ignites a compressed
    mixture of air and fuel to create the combustion stroke.

24. In a diesel engine, fuel is ignited purely by the heat
    generated from high compression, with no spark plug required.

25. The explosive force of combustion pushes a piston downward
    inside the cylinder.

26. A crankshaft converts the linear up-and-down motion of the
    pistons into rotational motion that eventually turns the
    wheels.

27. A camshaft, driven by the crankshaft via a timing belt or
    chain, controls the precise opening and closing of intake and
    exhaust valves.

28. Because combustion engines only produce useful power within a
    limited RPM range, cars need multi-speed transmissions to
    manage the tradeoff between torque and speed.

29. A clutch (in manual transmissions) or torque converter (in
    automatics) allows the engine to disengage from the wheels so
    the car can idle without stalling.

30. Turbochargers use exhaust gases to spin a turbine that forces
    additional compressed air into the engine, increasing power
    without significantly increasing engine size.

31. Superchargers achieve a similar power boost to turbochargers,
    but are mechanically driven directly by the engine's
    crankshaft rather than by exhaust gases.

32. Fuel injection systems, which precisely spray fuel into the
    engine, largely replaced older carburetor systems by the
    1990s for better efficiency and emissions control.

33. Catalytic converters use precious metals like platinum,
    palladium, and rhodium to chemically convert harmful exhaust
    gases into less harmful compounds before they exit the
    tailpipe.

34. A typical gasoline engine converts only about 20 to 35
    percent of the chemical energy in fuel into usable motion,
    with most of the rest lost as heat.

35. Engine cooling systems, including radiators and water pumps,
    exist specifically to manage the enormous heat generated by
    repeated combustion.

36. Engine oil is essential to reduce friction between the many
    moving metal parts inside a combustion engine, and it also
    helps carry heat away from critical components.

37. A V engine arranges its cylinders in two angled banks (like
    a V shape) sharing a single crankshaft, while an inline engine
    arranges all cylinders in a single row.

38. A W engine, used in some high-performance vehicles, combines
    multiple cylinder banks into a compact configuration resembling
    the letter W.

39. Diesel engines generally achieve better fuel efficiency than
    gasoline engines due to higher compression ratios and the
    energy density of diesel fuel.

40. Hybrid vehicles combine a traditional combustion engine with
    an electric motor and battery, using the electric system to
    improve efficiency and reduce fuel consumption, most famously
    popularized by the Toyota Prius, launched in Japan in 1997.


HOW ELECTRIC MOTORS & EVs WORK

41. Electric motors operate on electromagnetic principles first
    demonstrated by scientists like Michael Faraday in the 1830s.

42. In an electric motor, current flowing through coiled wires
    creates a magnetic field that interacts with magnets on a
    rotating shaft, causing it to spin.

43. Electric motors can deliver maximum torque instantly from a
    complete stop, which is why EVs typically feel quicker off
    the line than comparable gasoline cars.

44. Most EVs use a single-speed gear reduction instead of a
    traditional multi-speed transmission, since electric motors
    operate efficiently across a much wider range of speeds.

45. An EV's battery pack is typically made up of hundreds or
    thousands of individual lithium-ion cells wired together in
    modules.

46. A battery management system continuously monitors
    temperature, voltage, and charge balance across an EV's
    battery cells to keep them operating safely.

47. Regenerative braking allows an EV's electric motor to run in
    reverse as a generator during braking, recapturing kinetic
    energy and feeding it back into the battery.

48. Regenerative braking allows some EVs to be driven using
    "one-pedal driving," where lifting off the accelerator alone
    provides significant braking force.

49. Electric motors typically convert roughly 85 to 95 percent of
    electrical energy into motion, making them far more efficient
    than combustion engines.

50. Two of the most common electric motor designs used in EVs are
    permanent magnet synchronous motors and AC induction motors,
    the latter popularized in early Tesla models.

51. Lithium-ion batteries, the dominant EV battery type, were
    originally developed for consumer electronics like laptops
    and camcorders in the 1990s before being adapted for
    automotive use.

52. Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries have become
    increasingly popular in EVs because they are cheaper and more
    durable, though they generally offer somewhat lower energy
    density than nickel-based lithium-ion chemistries.

53. DC fast charging can recharge many modern EV batteries to
    around 80 percent capacity in roughly 20 to 40 minutes,
    depending on the vehicle and charger.

54. Most EV owners primarily charge overnight at home using
    slower Level 1 or Level 2 AC chargers rather than relying on
    public DC fast chargers.

55. EV batteries typically use liquid cooling systems to manage
    heat during both driving and fast charging.

56. Because EVs have no traditional exhaust system, transmission,
    or engine block, they generally have far fewer moving parts
    than gasoline cars, which can reduce long-term maintenance
    needs.

57. Many EVs feature a "frunk," or front trunk, a storage space
    made possible by the absence of a large combustion engine at
    the front of the car.

58. EV battery packs are typically mounted low in the vehicle's
    floor, which lowers the car's center of gravity and can
    improve handling and rollover resistance.

59. Cold weather can significantly reduce an EV's driving range,
    since heating the cabin and battery consumes energy that
    would otherwise go toward propulsion.

60. Public charging networks, including fast-charging corridors
    along highways, have expanded rapidly worldwide, with China
    alone adding over a million new public chargers in 2025.


MODERN MARKET, POLICY & MISCELLANEOUS

61. Global electric car sales surpassed 20 million units in 2025,
    accounting for roughly a quarter of all new car sales
    worldwide.

62. China accounts for more than half of all new EV sales
    globally, making it by far the largest electric vehicle
    market on Earth.

63. Norway has one of the highest EV adoption rates in the world,
    with electric vehicles representing the vast majority of new
    car sales in the country.

64. The United States' EV sales share has lagged behind China and
    Europe, and sales slowed further after changes to federal tax
    credits reduced financial incentives for buyers.

65. China produces the large majority of the world's EV battery
    cells, with companies like CATL and BYD dominating global
    battery manufacturing.

66. CATL, founded in 2011, has grown into the world's largest EV
    battery manufacturer by installed capacity.

67. Battery pack costs have fallen dramatically over the past
    decade due to manufacturing scale and improved chemistries,
    narrowing the price gap between EVs and gasoline cars.

68. Plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs) combine a battery
    and electric motor with a traditional combustion engine,
    allowing limited all-electric range alongside gasoline
    backup.

69. Fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEVs) generate electricity
    onboard through a chemical reaction between hydrogen and
    oxygen, emitting only water vapor, though they remain a much
    smaller segment of the market than battery electric vehicles.

70. The Toyota Mirai and Hyundai Nexo are among the most notable
    hydrogen fuel cell vehicles sold to consumers.

71. The European Union's tightening CO2 emissions standards have
    been a significant driver of EV adoption growth across
    European car markets.

72. Many governments around the world have announced future
    bans or phase-outs of new gasoline and diesel car sales,
    with target dates ranging from the 2030s to 2040s in various
    countries.

73. Tesla's Supercharger network, first launched in 2012, became
    one of the most extensive fast-charging networks in the
    world and has since been opened up to other automakers'
    vehicles in many regions.

74. The Combined Charging System (CCS) and Tesla's North American
    Charging Standard (NACS) are among the major EV charging
    connector standards used across different markets.

75. Range anxiety, the fear of running out of charge before
    reaching a charging station, has historically been cited as
    one of the biggest psychological barriers to EV adoption.

76. Modern EV battery ranges have increased substantially over
    the past decade, with many mainstream models now offering
    well over 250 miles (about 400 kilometers) of range on a
    single charge.

77. Automakers including Volkswagen, General Motors, Hyundai, and
    Ford have invested tens of billions of dollars collectively
    into dedicated EV platforms and battery manufacturing since
    the early 2020s.

78. Solid-state batteries, an emerging technology using solid
    rather than liquid electrolytes, are being researched by
    several automakers as a potential future upgrade offering
    higher energy density and improved safety.

79. Battery recycling has become an increasingly important
    industry as the first generation of mass-market EV batteries
    begins reaching end-of-life.

80. Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) technology allows some EVs to send
    stored electricity back into the power grid or into a home,
    turning parked cars into potential backup power sources.

81. The efficiency of an internal combustion engine is heavily
    affected by its compression ratio, which measures how much
    the air-fuel mixture is squeezed before ignition.

82. Formula 1 and other motorsports have historically served as
    testing grounds for combustion engine innovations that later
    trickled down into consumer vehicles, such as turbocharging
    and advanced fuel injection.

83. Electric motors are also used extensively outside of
    passenger cars, including in electric buses, trucks,
    scooters, bicycles, and increasingly in electric aircraft
    prototypes.

84. Diesel engines are widely used in freight trucks, ships, and
    heavy machinery due to their high torque output and fuel
    efficiency at heavy loads.

85. Ethanol and biodiesel are examples of biofuels that can be
    blended with or substituted for traditional gasoline and
    diesel in combustion engines with certain modifications.

86. Engine displacement, usually measured in liters, refers to
    the total volume swept by all the pistons in an engine and is
    a common way of comparing engine size across different cars.

87. Direct fuel injection, which sprays fuel straight into the
    combustion chamber rather than the intake manifold, has
    become common in modern gasoline engines for improved
    efficiency and power.

88. Cylinder deactivation technology allows some combustion
    engines to shut off a portion of their cylinders during light
    driving conditions to save fuel, then reactivate them when
    more power is needed.

89. Start-stop systems, now common in many gasoline and hybrid
    vehicles, automatically shut off the engine when the car is
    stationary, such as at a red light, to reduce fuel consumption
    and emissions.

90. The International Energy Agency projects that electric cars
    could reach roughly half of global new car sales by 2035,
    though the pace of that transition will depend heavily on
    battery costs, charging infrastructure, and government policy
    in different regions of the world.
